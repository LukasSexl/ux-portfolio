01At-a-Glance
The goal was to design a mobile application for a music festival. We were provided with a list of requirements the app should fullfill in the MoSCoW format. Creating the information architecture and a moodboard were the first steps. Then I made Lo-Fi and Hi-Fi Prototypes, which were improved through various methods of User Testing to arrive at a final product.
02Tasks
Distil requirements into an information architecture.
Create Mood Board.
Create Lo-Fi Wireframes, perform Usability Testing and iterate.
Use Lo-Fi and Mood Board as backbone for Hi-Fi Wireframes.
Perform Heuristic Evaluation and Desirability Test and iterate.
03Requirements
We were given a list of requirements in the MoSCoW format and should create a Site Map based on that to include all the necessary pages/information.
Basically there are 3 layers of depth.

I wanted to have a navigation bar that is always accessible with the most important elements: Home, Menu, Profile.

Because the app should serve as a companion to the user, I thought a customizable profile page is important to always be accessible. There they can see their Ticket and Favourite Artists.

The Menu should include 4 subpages: Line Up, Tickets, Timetable, News which will have more detailed information themselves.

And on the Home Page there will be an overview of featured artists and news, what the festival is about, quick access to the map and the weather forecast.

I tried to stick to familiar concepts of navigation for the user so they can focus on their goals.
04Mood Board
Next to giving myself a better understanding of the aesthetics of the festival application, we had some guidelines:
A colour palette of at least 3 colours
A general idea about typography
5 words that describe the general mood of your festival.

Since I didn’t know which genre to go for at first, I started skimming the magazines we were provided with for any inspiration.
I started to find some robots and this yellow colour that sparked my interest.
The more elements I collected the more I thought that my mood board gives a techno genre vibe.

And even though I have never been to a techno festival, I decided to take on the challenge.
05Lo-Fi
Using the Prototyping Tool Whimsical I created Lo-Fi Wireframes.
Testing:

I decided to do qualitative user testing. So I made a Test Protocol, thought of realistic tasks a user would do in the app and tested them on 5 subjects.

The tasks I decided for:
Navigate to the overview menu
Add an artist to your favourites
View your favourite artists
Buy a ticket
Read a news article

To document and better analyse the tests I filmed my users (with permission). Afterwards I asked follow up questions.

Eventually I iterated on the feedback I gathered.


Insights I could gather:
one of my testers had difficulties identifying where to click in the burger menu
the festival name was not apparent
the slider in “news” seemed more like a loading bar than a slider
the intended progress bar in the news article seemed more like a downloading bar
the extra weather page is not necessary since the little overview in the bottom is sufficient - this allows also a reduction in elements in the burger menu


The changes are already visible in the Whimsical board. I labled the screens I performed changes on with “before” and “after”.
06Hi-Fi
Using the basic structure and the aesthetics of the mood board I started creating the Hi-Fi version in Figma.

The goal was to give the app the look and feel of our mood board. I used the yellow colour and a dark palette to provide an underground, techno mood. Also, adding pictures of robots, some patterns and a glitched font for the headings adds to the feeling.



I decided against using the glitch font for the normal text to ensure legibility.

Some of my thoughts behind the design:
The homepage is made in a way to grab the users attention and to provide the most important elements first.
The user can discover the featured artists of the line-up to instantly let them recognize familiar faces invoking a positive feeling.
Then there is directly access to the most important pages: Tickets (in the companies interest) and timetable (in the user’s interest for quick navigation, while e.g. being on the festival).

For the line-up page I wanted to give the user again familiar elements to work with like the search bar, heart icons, artist cards, or filter option.
To make it obvious that the cards are clickable, which I was struggling a bit with, I ended up adding a gradient, and underlined “read more >>” to make it seem like a clickable link.

07Testing and Iterations
We used two types of testing methods each with 5 users: Heuristic Evaluation and Microsoft’s Desirability Toolkit


Heuristic Evaluation:

I gave my users realistic tasks they needed to complete. Eventually they should analyse the design by a list of Jakob Nielsen's Usability Heuristics.

Insights gained:
It’s not always easy to recover from clicking somewhere → more back buttons
The line-up page could use more colour
Map with stages is inconsistent
Would be good if artists on timetable are clickable for quicker navigation


Desirability Toolkit:

I gave my 5 users a randomized list of words to select from for the main pages of my app. Feedback I received the most pregnant is that the line up page is too boring and I should maybe add some more colour or a different structure.

Other than that the selected words are positive. In general I got that that the aesthetic design is not directly what I was hoping for but that the usability is good.


Changes I made:

The artists on the timetable are clickable
There are back arrows on every page
The Map design is now consistent
On the line-up page I added an accent colour and introduced a different layout that is not as repetitive while creating hierarchy too.
